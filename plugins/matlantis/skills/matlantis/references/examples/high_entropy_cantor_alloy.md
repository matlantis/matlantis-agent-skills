# Simulation of High Entropy Cantor Alloy (AlCoCrFeNi) Using LightPFP


## Abstract


Light Preferred Potential (LightPFP) is a machine learning interatomic potential that provides a powerful and efficient approach for modeling materials at the atomic scale. By leveraging large datasets of atomic configurations and energies from the universal machine learning Preferred Potential (PFP), LightPFP can accurately capture the complex interactions in various materials while offering significant computational speedups and enabling simulations at larger scales compared to the original PFP, let alone density functional theory (DFT) calculations. In this study, we employ the LightPFP model to investigate the high entropy Cantor alloy (AlCoCrFeNi), benchmarking its reliability for modeling multicomponent alloy systems. We construct a comprehensive dataset encompassing both bulk and grain boundary configurations to train the LightPFP potential. By utilizing this potential, we provide tools to enable unprecedented insights into the structure and dynamics of this complex alloy system through large-scale simulations of entire polycrystalline grains. The accuracy and transferability of the LightPFP potential are validated by comparing key properties, such as equations of state, phonon spectra, elastic properties, and molecular dynamics trajectories, against reference PFP calculations.


## Introduction


The Preferred Potential (PFP) is a powerful and versatile universal machine learning potential that has revolutionized atomistic simulations of materials. One of the key advantages of PFP is its remarkable computational efficiency, offering speedups of several orders of magnitude compared to traditional density functional theory (DFT) calculations [1]. Moreover, PFP does not require explicit training for specific materials systems, making it readily applicable to any combination of elements without the need for extensive data generation and potential fitting. This remarkable transferability enables PFP to model a wide range of materials, from elemental solids to complex multicomponent alloys and compounds.

Despite its many strengths, PFP has limitations. While significantly faster than DFT, its computational cost can still be prohibitive for simulations involving large system sizes or long timescales. Additionally, the size of the PFP model can impose practical constraints on the maximum simulation scale, typically limited to systems with up to tens of thousands of atoms.

To address these challenges, the Light Preferred Potential (LightPFP) framework has been developed. LightPFP leverages the vast dataset generated by the universal PFP to train specialized, non-universal machine learning potentials tailored to specific materials systems. By focusing on a narrower range of compositions and configurations, these potentials can achieve even higher computational efficiency and enable simulations at larger scales while retaining the accuracy of the PFP dataset.

However, as the number of elemental components in a material system increases, the complexity of the potential energy landscape grows exponentially, posing significant challenges for the development of reliable machine learning potentials. High entropy alloys (HEAs), a class of multicomponent alloys consisting of five or more principal elements in approximately equiatomic ratios, represent a formidable test case for the LightPFP approach.

In this work, we explore the application of LightPFP to the simulation of the Cantor alloy (AlCoCrFeNi), a prototypical HEA exhibiting exceptional mechanical, thermal, and chemical properties [2]. The Cantor alloy contains five elements and each element counts for 20% of the total composition. By leveraging the accuracy and efficiency of the LightPFP potential, we aim to benchmark its performance and reliability for modeling complex, multicomponent alloy systems, paving the way for future investigations and design of advanced structural materials.


## Results and discussion


### Datasets


The canter alloy comprises 5 elements: Al, Co, Cr, Fe, and Ni. It does not have long-range order, and each element randomly takes FCC lattice positions. To capture the structural characteristics, we create the training dataset using the "substitution" method provided in LightPFP. In simple terms, we read the initial structures in which only Al elements exist. Then, we randomly replace the Al elements with Co, Cr, Fe, or Ni elements, with a 20% probability for each. After that, we collect several training structures of this composition by conducting structural optimization and molecular dynamics simulations. To ensure the training dataset contains abundant structural information, we repeat the above steps many times.

Besides the bulk structure, we are also interested in simulating grain boundaries and whole polycrystalline grains. For this reason, the training dataset should contain both bulk, surface, and grain boundary structures. Thus, the "substitution" dataset generation protocol is performed not only with the perfect FCC lattice but also with several FCC grain boundaries and surfaces as the initial structures. The following grain boundaries are used: Σ3 60.0/\[1 1 1\] (1 1 1), Σ3 109.47/\[1 1 0\] (1 -1 -2), Σ3 180.0/\[1 1 1\] (-1 -1 0), Σ5 36.87/\[1 0 0\] (1 0 0), Σ5 53.13/\[1 0 0\] (0 -1 -3), Σ5 36.87/\[1 0 0\] (0 -2 1), Σ7 38.21/\[1 1 1\] (1 1 1), Σ7 38.21/\[1 1 1\] (3 -2 -1), Σ9 38.94/\[1 1 0\] (2 -2 -1), and Σ9 38.94/\[1 1 0\] (1 1 0). The Miller index of the surfaces are: (1 0 0), (1 1 0), (1 1 1), (2 1 0), (2 1 1), (2 2 1), (3 1 0), (3 1 1), (3 2 0), (3 2 1), (3 2 2), (3 3 1) and (3 3 2). The initial grain boundary and surface structures are generated with Pymatgen package [3].

Finally, a training dataset with 9,638 structures is created. The energy, forces, and stresses of these structures are labeled with PFP using the "PBE" calculation mode. The details are listed below. The dataset is randomly split into a training set (90%) and a testing set (10%). The script used to generate the training database can be found in the Script and Code section.

#### Equation of states


We calculated the equation of state of AlCoCrFeNi using the testing structure mentioned above. The equation of state was calculated in the following way: From the initial structure, we evenly compressed or stretched the cell length along the x, y, and z directions by -5% to +5%. Then, the atomic positions and cell shape were optimized with the constraint that the volume remained constant. Finally, we obtained the relationship between volume and potential energy.

The calculation was performed with both PFP and LightPFP using the same initial structure. The comparison of the results is plotted in Fig 2. From the equation of state, we can obtain the equilibrium volume, which is the minimum point of the curve, to be 11.510 Å&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;/atom and 11.511 Å&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;/atoms by LightPFP and PFP, respectively. The bulk moduli can also be obtained by fitting the Volume-Energy curve with the SJ equation of state [4]. The bulk moduli are estimated to be 165.313 GPa and 166.730 GPa by LightPFP and PFP, respectively.

#### Grain boundary energy


The grain boundary energy is calculated using equation (1). Since many grain boundary structures are already included in the training dataset, we chose a large-angle twist grain boundary, which was not included in the training dataset, as the testing case. The grain boundary index is Σ13 22.62/\[1 0 0\] (1 0 0). The calculation process is listed as follows: (1) create the grain boundary structure with an FCC lattice and grain boundary rotation axis, angle, and plane information (the FCC lattice has a lattice constant of 3.584 Å, which is obtained from the equation of state above); (2) randomly place Al, Cr, Fe, Co, and Ni into the lattice; (3) optimize the structure and obtain the energy of the grain boundary structure; (4) calculate the energy of the bulk structure. Since the target system is a solid solution, and each element is randomly distributed, we use large grain boundaries and bulk structures to avoid the randomness of the calculation results.


$$\gamma_{EG}=\frac{E_{GB}-n_{GB}E_{\text{bulk}}}{2A_{GB}} \tag{1}$$


The grain boundary energies are estimated to be 0.128 eV/Å&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt; and 0.131 eV/Å&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt; with LightPFP and PFP, respectively. The calculation results indicate that LightPFP perfectly reproduces the PFP results in the calculation of a large Σ grain boundary that was not included in the training dataset.

### Dataset collection

* To collect a diverse set of training structures representing the entire polycrystalline grain, crystal, surface, and grain boundary structures are generated.

* The method to generate initial surface and grain boundary structures can be found in the `./assets/high_entropy_cantor_alloy_datasets/initial_structures.ipynb` notebook.

* The "substitution" section in the control files (crystal.json, boundary.json, and surface.json) specifies the random replacement of atoms in the initial structures. The elements to be substituted are given in "elements": [27, 24, 26, 28], which correspond to the atomic numbers of Co, Cr, Fe, and Ni, respectively. The "possibility": [0.2, 0.2, 0.2, 0.2] list provides the probabilities for substituting each element, set to equal values of 0.2 in this case.
The "md": true option is enabled, allowing for the collection of additional training structures during molecular dynamics (MD) simulations after the initial substitution. The MD simulation settings are controlled by the following parameters:
    * sampling_temp: Temperature for the MD simulations
    * sampling_steps: Number of simulation steps
    * sampling_interval: Interval at which structures are sampled
The entire process of substitution and MD simulation is repeated `n_sample` times to generate multiple training structures.


* In this example, the LightPFP model is used for MD simulations up to 1000K. To ensure that the configurations generated during the MD simulations fall within the model's learned capacity, the highest sampling temperature is set to at least 1.5 times the MD temperature, which is 1500K in this case. Therefore, the training dataset is generated from MD simulations at 500K, 1000K, and 1500K.

### Equation of state
* The initial structure `assets/high_entropy_cantor_alloy/AlCrFeCoNi.cif` is prepared a by randomly placing 5 elements on the FCC lattice sites. The structure contains 256 atoms.


```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np
from ase import Atoms, units
from ase.io import read
from ase.eos import EquationOfState
from ase.optimize import FIRE
from ase.filters import FrechetCellFilter
from tabulate import tabulate
from tqdm import tqdm

from light_pfp_client import Estimator, ASECalculator

from pfp_api_client.pfp.calculators.ase_calculator import ASECalculator as PFPASECalculator
from pfp_api_client.pfp.estimator import Estimator as PFPEstimator


# This is the example model ID, but please replace it with your trained model ID when you trained by yourself.
model_id = "examplesv1hienca"
calc_lpfp = ASECalculator(Estimator(model_id=model_id, book_keeping=True))
calc_pfp = PFPASECalculator(PFPEstimator(model_version="v5.0.0", calc_mode="PBE"))


def eos(atoms, calc, min_scale: float = 0.95, max_scale: float = 1.05, interval: float = 0.01):
    atoms_opt = atoms.copy()
    n_atoms = len(atoms_opt)
    atoms_opt.calc = calc
    fcf = FrechetCellFilter(atoms_opt, constant_volume=True)
    opt = FIRE(fcf, logfile=None)
    opt.run(fmax=0.01, steps=10000)
    cell = atoms_opt.get_cell()
    V = []
    E = []
    for scale in tqdm(np.arange(min_scale, max_scale + 1e-5, interval)):
        atoms_v = atoms_opt.copy()
        atoms_v.calc = calc
        atoms_v.set_cell(cell * scale, scale_atoms=True)
        fcf = FrechetCellFilter(atoms_v, constant_volume=True)
        opt = FIRE(fcf, logfile=None)
        opt.run(fmax=0.01, steps=10000)
        V.append(atoms_v.get_volume()/n_atoms)
        E.append(atoms_v.get_potential_energy()/n_atoms)
    return V, E


atoms = read("assets/high_entropy_cantor_alloy/AlCrFeCoNi.cif")
V_lpfp, E_lpfp = eos(atoms, calc_lpfp)
V_pfp, E_pfp = eos(atoms, calc_pfp)

eos_pfp = EquationOfState(V_pfp, E_pfp)
v0_pfp, e0_pfp, B_pfp = eos_pfp.fit()
eos_lpfp = EquationOfState(V_lpfp, E_lpfp)
v0_lpfp, e0_lpfp, B_lpfp = eos_lpfp.fit()

res = [
    ["B", B_lpfp / units.GPa, B_pfp / units.GPa, np.abs(B_lpfp - B_pfp) / units.GPa, np.abs(B_lpfp - B_pfp) / B_pfp],
    ["V0", v0_lpfp, v0_pfp, np.abs(v0_lpfp - v0_pfp), np.abs(v0_lpfp - v0_pfp) / v0_pfp],
]

print(tabulate(res, headers=["", "LightPFP", "PFP", "err", "rel err"]))

plt.plot(V_pfp, E_pfp, marker="o", markersize=5, label="PFP")
plt.plot(V_lpfp, E_lpfp, marker="o", markersize=5, label="LightPFP")
plt.xlabel("Volume (Å$^3$/atom)")
plt.ylabel("Energy (eV/atom)")
plt.legend()
plt.savefig("eos.png")
```

def lattice_calc(atoms, calc):
    atoms_opt = atoms.copy()
    atoms_opt.calc = calc
    fcf = FrechetCellFilter(atoms_opt)
    opt = FIRE(fcf, logfile=None)
    opt.run(fmax=0.01, steps=10000)
    cell = atoms_opt.get_cell()
    volume = atoms_opt.get_volume()

def phonon_calc(atoms, calc, supercell):
    atoms_opt = atoms.copy()
    atoms_opt.calc = calc
    fcf = FrechetCellFilter(atoms_opt)
    fire = FIRE(fcf, logfile=None)

### Molecular dynamics simulation

#### Define MD workflow


```python
import os
import json
import numpy as np
from time import perf_counter

from ase import units
from ase.io import Trajectory
from ase.optimize import FIRE
from ase.md.npt import NPT
from ase.md.langevin import Langevin


class PrintDyn:
    def __init__(self, dyn, logfile=None):
        self.dyn = dyn
        self.st = perf_counter()
        self.logfile = logfile
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "w") as fd:
                fd.write("# step E_tot E_pot density T elapsed_time\n")
    def __call__(self):
        dyn = self.dyn
        msg = (
            f"{dyn.get_number_of_steps(): 6d} {atoms.get_total_energy():.3f} {atoms.get_potential_energy():.3f} "
            f"{atoms.get_masses().sum() / units.kg / atoms.get_volume() * 1e27:.5f} "
            f"{atoms.get_temperature():.1f} {perf_counter() - self.st:.2f}"
        )
        print(msg)
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "a") as fd:
                fd.write(msg+"\n")


def run_md(atoms, save_dir, temperature, init_steps, steps):
    print(f"Number of atoms: {len(atoms)}")
    os.makedirs(f"{save_dir}", exist_ok=True)

    fire = FIRE(atoms)
    fire.run(fmax=0.1, steps=100) # set max_step = 100
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/opt.cif")

    md = Langevin(
        atoms, 
        timestep=units.fs, 
        temperature_K=300.0,
        friction=0.1
    )
    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md)
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=init_steps)
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/start.cif")
    traj = Trajectory(f"{save_dir}/md.traj", "w", atoms = atoms)
    md = NPT(
        atoms, 
        timestep=units.fs, 
        temperature_K=temperature,
        externalstress=101325 * units.Pascal,
        mask = np.eye(3),
        ttime = 20.0 * units.fs,
        pfactor = 2e6 * units.GPa*(units.fs**2),
        logfile =None,
    )

    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md, logfile=f"{save_dir}/md.log")
    md.attach(traj.write, interval=10000)
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=steps)
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/final.cif")

```

def get_distance_matrix(atoms):
    structure = AseAtomsAdaptor.get_structure(atoms)
    return structure.distance_matrix
    

def get_rdf(traj, cutoff, bin_width):
    steps = len(traj)
    n_atoms = len(traj[0])
    elements = traj[0].get_atomic_numbers()
    elements_unique = np.unique(elements)
    n_elements = len(elements_unique)
    rdf_bins = np.append(np.arange(0, cutoff, bin_width), cutoff)
    bin_center = (rdf_bins[1:] + rdf_bins[:-1]) / 2.0
    rdf_values = np.zeros([n_elements, n_elements, len(rdf_bins)-1])
    for atoms in tqdm(traj):
        distance = get_distance_matrix(atoms)
        volume = atoms.get_volume()
        for j in range(n_elements):
            for k in range(n_elements):
                indices_1 = elements == elements_unique[j]
                indices_2 = elements == elements_unique[k]
                rho = np.sum(indices_2) / volume
                if j == k:
                    triu_indices = np.triu_indices(np.sum(indices_1), k=1)
                    dist = distance[indices_1][:, indices_2][triu_indices]
                    bin_counts= np.histogram(dist, rdf_bins)[0] * 2 / np.sum(indices_1)
                else:
                    bin_counts= np.histogram(distance[indices_1][:, indices_2], rdf_bins)[0]  / np.sum(indices_1)
                volume_shell = 4.0 * np.pi * bin_center**2 * bin_width
                rdf_values[j, k] += bin_counts / volume_shell / rho 
    rdf_values /= steps
    return bin_center, rdf_values

def random_replace(atoms, elements):
    atoms_c = atoms.copy()
    assert len(elements) == 4
    ind = np.random.random(len(atoms_c))
    for i, e in enumerate(elements):
        atoms_c.numbers[(ind &gt; 0.0) &amp; (ind &lt; 0.2)] = e
    return atoms_c


def get_energy(atoms, calc, cell_opt=False):
    atoms_c = atoms.copy()
    atoms_c.calc = calc
    if cell_opt:
        fcf = FrechetCellFilter(atoms_c)
        opt = FIRE(fcf, logfile=None)
    else:
        opt = FIRE(atoms_c, logfile=None)
    opt.run(fmax=0.05, steps=100000)
    return atoms_c.get_potential_energy()


elements = [24, 26, 27, 28]
