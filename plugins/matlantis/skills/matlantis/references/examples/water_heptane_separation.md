# Molecular Dynamics Simulations of Liquid-Liquid Phase Separation Using LightPFP

## Abstract

In this example, we investigate the phenomenon of liquid-liquid phase separation using LightPFP. LightPFP models are constructed for water and heptane, and the reliability of each model is validated by comparing calculated properties like density and diffusion coefficient with PFP results. Large-scale molecular dynamics simulations are then performed on a system containing 17,280 water and 2,160 heptane molecules. After 0.8 nanosecond of simulation time, spontaneous liquid-liquid phase separation is clearly observed, with the two immiscible liquids forming distinct phases. The ability to efficiently model and directly observe the phase separation process provides valuable molecular-level insight into this fundamental phenomenon. 


## Introduction

Modeling and simulating the process of liquid-liquid phase separation is an important problem with broad relevance across chemical, biological, and materials sciences. However, capturing this phenomenon through molecular simulation poses significant challenges. First, an accurate description of the intermolecular interactions is required, which consequently requires high-quality interatomic potentials. Second, a sufficiently large simulation size and long timescales are needed to observe the spontaneous separation into distinct liquid phases, quickly exceeding the limits of ab initio methods like density functional theory (DFT) and is also challenging for PFP.

In this work, we utilize LightPFP to efficiently model and directly observe liquid-liquid phase separation. LightPFP is based on the Moment Tensor Potentials method, and is trained from reference datasets obtained from PFP simulations. LightPFP potentials can simulate 1~2 orders of magnitude faster on much larger system sizes compared to PFP itself, while still retaining the high fidelity of PFP training data.

By constructing reliable LightPFP models for water and heptane, two immiscible liquids, we are able to perform nanoseconds simulations on systems containing thousands of molecules. This allows us to capture the full liquid-liquid de-mixing process and characterize the molecular interactions driving the phase separation behavior.


## LightPFP model generation

### Dataset Collection

To obtain a comprehensive training dataset for constructing the LightPFP models, three main types of structures were included: bulk water, bulk heptane, and water/heptane mixtures.

For the bulk liquid structures, initial configurations were prepared using a "liquid generator" method, where N molecules were randomly placed in a simulation box with a density close to the experimental value, followed by a molecule-orientation-adjust procedure to resolve any atomic overlaps. Two different initial structures were created for both bulk water and bulk heptane to enhance diversity of sampled structures. These configurations served as starting points for molecular dynamics (MD) simulations in the NPT ensemble at 300, 350, 400, and 450 K. Due to the boiling points of water and heptane being below 400 K, simulations at 400 and 450 K were conducted at an elevated pressure of 10 bar. Additional NVT-MD simulations were performed at 500, 1000, and 1500 K to sample a broader range of configurations. For heptane, structures with compressed and expanded densities were also generated by applying ±3% strains along x, y, and z directions to improve sampling of density fluctuations. In total, 1700 bulk water structures (408,000 atoms) and 2306 bulk heptane structures (1,325,950 atoms) were collected.

The water/heptane mixture dataset consisted of two subsets: homogeneous mixtures and interfacial configurations. For the mixed structures, systems contains different number of water/heptane molecules, i.e. 140/3, 120/5, 80/10, 40/15, and 20/18, to capture the variation in local environments during the de-mixing process. Interfacial structures were created by filling one half of the box with water and the other with heptane to sample the liquid-liquid interface and associated interface energy. In all, 10,064 homogeneous mixtures and interfacial configurations containing 5,546,337 atoms were obtained.

### Model Training

The LightPFP Moment Tensor potentials for water, heptane, and their mixtures were trained using the ORGANIC pre-trained model as the starting point. The ORGANIC model was pre-trained on a broad dataset spanning many different organic molecules and could handle 11 elements, including H, C, N, O, F, Si, P, S, Cl, Br, and I, while incorporating D3 van der Waals corrections. Its hyperparameters were set to a maximum level of 8, a moment mu cost of 1, and a 6 Å cutoff distance.

The dataset was randomly split into a training set (90%) and a test set (10%). The ORGANIC pre-trained model was then further fine-tuned on the training set. The accuracy of the trained LightPFP potentials was evaluated by comparing predicted energy, force, and stress to the reference PFP data in the test set. As shown in Figure 1, the potentials achieve excellent prediction accuracy with mean absolute errors of 0.606 meV/atom for energies, 58.509 meV/Å for forces, and 0.2 meV/Å&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt; for stresses.

## LightPFP Model Validation

To assess the accuracy of the trained LightPFP models, we performed 50 ps molecular dynamics simulations in the NPT ensemble at 300 K for bulk water (2160 molecules) and bulk heptane (270 molecules) systems and compared calculated properties to the reference PFP and experimental data.

The radial distribution functions (RDFs) for water and heptane from LightPFP simulations showed excellent agreement with PFP results (Figure 2). Angular distribution functions for characteristic bonds and angles like H-O-H, H-C-H, H-C-C, C-C-C also matched closely between LightPFP and PFP (Figure 3).

### Basic Thermodynamic Properties

For water, LightPFP predicted the density as 0.98 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt; (PFP: 0.97 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;, Expt: 0.99 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;) and diffusion coefficient as 4.32 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-6&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s (PFP: 3.59 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-6&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s, Expt: 2.3 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-5&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s). The diffusion coefficient matches PFP but is smaller than the experimental value, a known limitation of the underlying PBE density functional. Notably, some research indicates that the water diffusion coefficient is estimated to be 3-6 × 10&lt;sup&gt;-6&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s using PBE DFT, which aligns well with our LightPFP/PFP results [1]. 

For heptane, LightPFP slightly overestimated the density as 0.72 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt; (PFP: 0.69 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;, Expt: 0.68 g/cm&lt;sup&gt;3&lt;/sup&gt;) but showed better agreement for diffusion (4.72 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-5&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s vs PFP: 6.07 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-5&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s, Expt: 3.12 x 10&lt;sup&gt;-5&lt;/sup&gt; cm&lt;sup&gt;2&lt;/sup&gt;/s), thermal conductivity (140.3 mW/m-K vs PFP: 121.7 mW/m-K, Expt: 120.9 mW/m-K), while overestimating the viscosity (0.816 cP vs PFP: 0.556 cP, Expt: 0.388 cP).

Overall, the LightPFP models capture the structural and basic thermodynamic properties of water and heptane reasonably well, with PFP showing better quantitative agreement with experiments as expected from the higher-level reference data. The slight deviations in LightPFP for dynamics and transport properties like diffusion and viscosity suggest that further refinements in the model form may be beneficial to improve the accuracy for quantities sensitive to collective molecular motions and many-body effects.

Potential areas for improvement include increasing the maximum representable level or rank of the Moment Tensor potential to describe more complex many-body interactions. Additionally, incorporating nuclear quantum effects through path-integral or centroid molecular dynamics could help better capture the highly quantum-natured behavior of light molecules like water. Explicit electronic polarization terms may also be needed to fully reproduce properties like dielectric constants.

It is worth noting that the LightPFP models were trained on a broad configurational dataset sampled from bulk liquid phases under ambient conditions. Their performance could potentially be further optimized by including more targeted sampling from extremes of state points (e.g. supercritical, interfacial) relevant for the specific application of interest. Overall, this validation demonstrates LightPFP as a promising approach to achieve an optimum balance of accuracy and computational efficiency suitable for modeling challenging molecular systems and phenomena like liquid-liquid phase separation.

## Large-Scale Simulation of Liquid-Liquid Phase Separation

To demonstrate the capability of LightPFP for modeling phase separation phenomena, we performed a large-scale molecular dynamics simulation on a system containing 17,280 water molecules and 2,160 heptane molecules, totaling 101,520 atoms. The initial configuration had water and heptane homogeneously mixed.

The simulation was run for 0.8 nanoseconds with a 1 fs timestep under NPT conditions at 300 K using the LightPFP water/heptane mixture model. The total simulation required 72.3 hours. Visualization and analysis were performed using the OVITO software [1].

Figure 4 shows representative snapshots of the system evolution over the 0.8 ns trajectory, with the water/heptane interface indicated by the silver isosurface. We directly observe the spontaneous de-mixing of the two immiscible liquids to form distinct water-rich and heptane-rich phases over the course of the simulation.

To quantify the de-mixing process, we calculated the total interfacial surface area between the two phases as a function of time, plotted in Figure 5. The surface area is seen to continuously decrease as the phases separate, consistent with the minimization of the high interfacial energy penalty.

While the current 0.8 ns timescale is insufficient to fully equilibrate the system into a perfectly flat liquid-liquid interface, these results clearly demonstrate the ability of LightPFP to efficiently model and provide molecular-level details into the liquid-liquid phase separation process for systems containing thousands of molecules. Longer simulations would be expected to produce a stable, minimized interface configuration. This large-scale modeling capability can provide key insights into the thermodynamics, kinetics and molecular driving forces underlying phase separation in multi-component liquid systems relevant across chemical, biological and materials applications.

# Conclusion

We demonstrated the application of the LightPFP machine learning molecular simulation approach to model liquid-liquid phase separation. Reliable LightPFP models were constructed for water, heptane, and their mixtures by training on a large reference dataset from PFP simulations. The LightPFP models accurately reproduced structural properties and basic thermodynamics of the bulk liquids, with some deviations in dynamical quantities.

Crucially, LightPFP enabled large-scale simulations of up to 100,000 atoms to directly observe the spontaneous de-mixing of water and heptane over nanosecond timescales. The time evolution of the interfacial surface area quantified the separation kinetics driven by minimizing the high interfacial energy.

While PFP provides higher quantitative accuracy, LightPFP allows simulations at unprecedented scales by bridging accuracy and computational efficiency. Further model refinements can extend LightPFP's accuracy to more complex properties. Overall, LightPFP presents a powerful approach for exploring liquid-liquid phase separation across chemical, materials and biological applications governed by this phenomenon.

### Define MD workflow
* Define the function to perform MD simulation


```python
import os
import numpy as np
from time import perf_counter

from ase import units
from ase.io import Trajectory
from ase.optimize import FIRE
from ase.md.npt import NPT
from ase.md.langevin import Langevin

class PrintDyn:
    def __init__(self, dyn, logfile=None):
        self.dyn = dyn
        self.st = perf_counter()
        self.logfile = logfile
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "w") as fd:
                fd.write("# step E_tot E_pot density T elapsed_time\n")
    def __call__(self):
        dyn = self.dyn
        msg = (
            f"{dyn.get_number_of_steps(): 6d} {atoms.get_total_energy():.3f} {atoms.get_potential_energy():.3f} "
            f"{atoms.get_masses().sum() / units.kg / atoms.get_volume() * 1e27:.5f} "
            f"{atoms.get_temperature():.1f} {perf_counter() - self.st:.2f}"
        )
        print(msg)
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "a") as fd:
                fd.write(msg+"\n")


def run_md(atoms, save_dir, temperature, init_steps, steps):
    print(f"Number of atoms: {len(atoms)}")

    os.makedirs(f"{save_dir}", exist_ok=True)

    fire = FIRE(atoms)
    fire.run(fmax=0.1, steps=100) # set max_step = 100
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/opt.cif")

    md = Langevin(
        atoms, 
        timestep=units.fs, 
        temperature_K=300.0,
        friction=0.1
    )
    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md)
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=init_steps)
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/start.cif")

    traj = Trajectory(f"{save_dir}/md.traj", "w", atoms = atoms)
    md = NPT(
        atoms, 
        timestep=units.fs, 
        temperature_K=temperature,
        externalstress=101325 * units.Pascal,
        mask = np.eye(3),
        ttime = 20.0 * units.fs,
        pfactor = 2e6 * units.GPa*(units.fs**2),
        logfile =None,
    )

    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md, logfile=f"{save_dir}/md.log")
    md.attach(traj.write, interval=1000)
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=steps)
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/final.cif")

```

### Mean square displacement


```python
import numpy as np
from ase.io import Trajectory


def get_msd(traj):
    steps = len(traj)
    n_atoms = len(traj[0])
    positions = np.array([atoms.get_positions() for atoms in traj])
    msd_atoms = np.zeros([steps, n_atoms, 3])
    for i in range(1, steps):
        disp_2 = (positions[i:] - positions[:-i]) ** 2
        msd_atoms[i] = np.mean(disp_2, axis=0)
    return msd_atoms.mean(axis=1)


def get_diffusion_coefficient(traj, interval=1.0, init=0):
    """
    args:
        traj: The input trajectory.
        interval: The time interval between two snapshot, unit ps.
        init: Skip the first `init` snapshots for the fitting of MSD slop.
    """
    msd = get_msd(traj)
    time = np.arange(len(traj)) * interval
    slope = np.polyfit(time[init:], msd.mean(axis=1)[init:], 1)[1]   # A^2/ps
    d = slope/6 * 1e-4 # cm^2/s
    return d
```

## Non equilibrium Molecular dynamics simulation
* The Non-equilibrium MD simulation method is defined to calculate the [viscosity](https://journals.aps.org/pre/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevE.59.4894) and [thermal conductivity](https://pubs.aip.org/aip/jcp/article-abstract/106/14/6082/181799/A-simple-nonequilibrium-molecular-dynamics-method?redirectedFrom=fulltext) for organic liquid.
* Note that the same method is implemented in the matlantis-features, i.e. `ComplexNEMDViscosityFeature` and `ComplexNEMDThermalConductivityFeature`. However, the long MD trajectories must be saved for the post analysis in matlantis-features, which is not feasible for the large-scale long NEMD simulations. Thus, we provide a new implementation here.



### Define Non-equilibrium MD function
* Define the function to perform NEMD simulation


```python
import warnings
from typing import Tuple, Optional
from packaging import version

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
if version.parse(np.__version__) &lt; version.parse("2.0.0"):
    from numpy.lib.polynomial import RankWarning
else:
    from numpy.exceptions import RankWarning

from ase import Atoms

class RNEMDExtension:
    """Extension class for the reverse non-equilibrium MD simulation."""

    def __init__(
        self,
        md: Dynamics,
        rnemd_type: str,
        n_slab: int,
        n_swap: int = 1,
        swap_interval: int = 1,
        log_interval: int = 1,
        logfile: Optional[str] = None
    ):
        """Initialize an instance.
        Args:
            rnemd_type (str): Name of the rNEMD calculation type
            ("viscosity" or "thermal_conductivity").
            n_slab (int): Number of the slabs.
        """
        assert n_slab % 2 == 0
        assert rnemd_type in ["viscosity", "thermal_conductivity"]
        self.md = md
        self.rnemd_type = rnemd_type
        self.n_slab = n_slab
        self.n_swap = n_swap
        self.swap_interval = swap_interval
        self.log_interval = log_interval
        self.logfile = logfile
        self.exchange = 0.0
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "w") as fw:
                fw.write("# rNEMD log file\n")
                fw.write("# time steps exchanged_momenta\n")

    def __call__(self) -&gt; None:
        steps = self.md.nsteps
        if steps % self.swap_interval == 0:
            self._swap()

        # save log
        if self.logfile is not None and steps % self.log_interval == 0:
            with open(self.logfile, "a") as fw:
                fw.write(f"{self.md.nsteps} {self.exchange}\n")

    def _swap(self) -&gt; None:
        atoms = self.md.atoms

        # get atoms dynamics information
        atn = atoms.get_atomic_numbers()
        momenta = atoms.get_momenta()
        kinetic = 0.5 * np.sum(momenta**2, axis=1) / atoms.get_masses()

        # get slab index
        slab = _get_slab_index(atoms, self.n_slab)

        # Identify unique elements in atoms
        elements = np.unique(atn)

        for e in elements:
            # Identify exchange atom in slab 0
            ind = np.where((slab == 0) &amp; (atn == e))[0]
            if len(ind) == 0:
                raise ValueError(f"Cannot find any element {e} inside slab 0. Please reduce 'n_slab' ")

            if self.rnemd_type == "viscosity":
                sort_id = momenta[ind, 0].argsort()[::-1]
            else:
                sort_id = kinetic[ind].argsort()[::-1]
            exchange_id_0 = ind[sort_id[:self.n_swap]]

            # Identify exchange atom in slab m
            ind = np.where((slab == self.n_slab//2) &amp; (atn == e))[0]
            if len(ind) == 0:
                raise ValueError(
                    f"Cannot find any element {e} inside slab {self.n_slab // 2}. Please reduce 'n_slab' "
                )

            if self.rnemd_type == "viscosity":
                sort_id = momenta[ind, 0].argsort()
            else:
                sort_id = kinetic[ind].argsort()
            exchange_id_1 = ind[sort_id[:self.n_swap]]
            
            for x0, x1 in zip(exchange_id_0, exchange_id_1):
                # Accumulate exchanged kinetic energy &amp; swap
                if self.rnemd_type == "viscosity":
                    self.exchange += momenta[x0, 0] - momenta[x1, 0]
                    momenta[[x0, x1], 0] = momenta[[x1, x0], 0]
                else:
                    self.exchange += kinetic[x0] - kinetic[x1]
                    momenta[[x0, x1]] = momenta[[x1, x0]]

        # update momenta
        atoms.set_momenta(momenta)
        

class LogExtension:
    def __init__(
        self,
        md: Dynamics,
        n_slab: int,
        freq: int,
        interval: int,
        logfile: Optional[str] = None,
    ):
        assert n_slab % 2 == 0
        assert interval &gt;= freq
        self.md = md
        
        self.n_slab = n_slab
        self.freq = freq
        self.interval = interval
        self.logfile = logfile

        self._n_step = 0
        self.z_position_collect = []
        self.profile_collect = []
        self.count_collect = []
        self.area_collect = []
        if self.logfile is not None:
            with open(self.logfile, "w") as fw:
                pass

    def __call__(self) -&gt; None:
        atoms = self.md.atoms
        steps = self.md.nsteps
        if steps % self.freq == 0:
            z_position, profile, count, area = self._get_profile(atoms)
            self.z_position_collect.append(z_position)
            self.profile_collect.append(profile)
            self.count_collect.append(count)
            self.area_collect.append(area)
        if steps % self.interval == 0:
            z_position_ave = np.mean(self.z_position_collect, axis=0)
            profile_ave = np.mean(self.profile_collect, axis=0)
            count_ave = np.mean(self.count_collect, axis=0)
            area_ave = np.mean(self.area_collect)
            n_chunk = len(self.z_position_collect)
            self.z_position_collect = []
            self.profile_collect = []
            self.count_collect = []
            self.area_collect = []
            if self.logfile is not None:
                with open(self.logfile, "a") as fw:
                    fw.write(f"# {steps} {n_chunk} {area_ave:10.3f}\n")
                    for i, (r, p, n) in enumerate(zip(z_position_ave, profile_ave, count_ave)):
                        fw.write(f"    {i:5d} {n:10.3f} {r:10.3f} {p:10.6f} \n")
        
    def _get_profile(self, atoms: Atoms) -&gt; Tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray, np.ndarray]:
        raise NotImplementedError


class VelocityLogExtension(LogExtension):
    def _get_profile(self, atoms: Atoms) -&gt; Tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray, np.ndarray]:
        # get velocity
        vx = atoms.get_velocities()[:, 0]

        # get cell shape
        cell = atoms.get_cell()
        Zmax = cell.array[:, 2].max()
        area = cell.volume / Zmax
        thickness = Zmax / self.n_slab

        # get slab index
        slab = _get_slab_index(atoms, self.n_slab)
        
        z_position = [(i+0.5) * thickness for i in range(self.n_slab)]
        profile = []
        count = []
        for i in range(self.n_slab):
            ind = np.where(slab == i)[0]
            profile.append(vx[ind].mean())
            count.append(len(ind))
        return z_position, profile, count, area


class TemperatureLogExtension(LogExtension):
    def _get_profile(self, atoms: Atoms) -&gt; Tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray, np.ndarray]:
        # get momenta and masses
        momenta = atoms.get_momenta()
        masses = atoms.get_masses()
        kinetic = np.sum(momenta ** 2, axis=1) / masses / 2.0 

        # get cell shape
        cell = atoms.get_cell()
        Zmax = cell.array[:, 2].max()
        area = cell.volume / Zmax
        thickness = Zmax / self.n_slab

        # get slab index
        slab = _get_slab_index(atoms, self.n_slab)
        
        z_position = [(i+0.5) * thickness for i in range(self.n_slab)]
        profile = []
        count = []
        for i in range(self.n_slab):
            ind = np.where(slab == i)[0]
            profile.append(kinetic[ind].mean() / 1.5 / units.kB)
            count.append(len(ind))
        return z_position, profile, count, area


def fit_slope(x: np.ndarray, y: np.ndarray) -&gt; Tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray]:
    """Perform the linear fitting for the post rNEMD analysis.
    Args:
        x (np.ndarray): X axis data.
        y (np.ndarray): Y axis data.
    Returns:
        tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray] :
          Result of the linear fitting (slope and slope std).
    """
    with warnings.catch_warnings():
        warnings.simplefilter("error", category=RankWarning)
        try:
            (slope, intercept), cov = np.polyfit(x, y, 1, cov=True)
        except RankWarning:
            raise ValueError(
                "This exception usually occurs because the simulation box is divided "
                "into too few slabs (n_slab &lt;= 8). "
                "Please increase the number of slabs 'n_slab' when performing NEMD simulation. "
            )
    slope_std, intercept_std = np.sqrt(np.diag(cov))
    return slope, slope_std


def get_property(
    rnemd_log: str, profile_log: str, timestep: float, init_steps: int = 0, final_steps: Optional[int] = None
) -&gt; float:
    """Calculate the viscosity from the rNEMD simulation.
    Args:
        rnemd_log (str): Path to the rNEMD log file.
        profile_log (str): Path to the profile log file.
        init_steps (int): Number of initial steps for the rNEMD simulation.
    Returns:
        float: Viscosity value.
    """

    # read the rNEMD log file
    rnemd_data = np.loadtxt(rnemd_log)
    
    # read the profile log file
    profile_data, step, area = _parse_profile(profile_log)

    # skip initial part
    assert np.all(np.array(step) == rnemd_data[:,0])
    if final_steps is None:
        ind = step &gt;= init_steps
    else:
        ind = (step &gt;= init_steps) &amp; (step &lt;= final_steps)

    exchanged = rnemd_data[ind, 1]
    profile = profile_data[ind, :, 2:]
    step = step[ind]
    area = area[ind]

    # wrap profile
    n_slab = profile.shape[1]
    x = profile[:, : n_slab//2 + 1, 0]
    y = profile[:, : n_slab//2 + 1, 1]
    for i in range(1, n_slab//2):
        y[:, i] += profile[:, -i, 1]
    y[:, 1 : n_slab // 2] /= 2.0

    # calculate viscosity
    t = (step[-1] - step[0]) * timestep
    x_ = x.mean(axis=0)
    y_ = y.mean(axis=0)
    area_ = area.mean()
    slope, _ = fit_slope(x_[2:-2], y_[2:-2])
    prop = (exchanged[-1] - exchanged[0]) / 2.0 / t / area_ / slope
    return prop


def _get_slab_index(atoms: Atoms, n_slab: int) -&gt; np.ndarray:
    atoms_c = atoms.copy()
    cell = atoms_c.get_cell()
    Zmax = cell.array[:, 2].max()
    atoms_c.wrap()
    z = atoms_c.get_positions()[:, 2]
    z[z&gt;Zmax] -= Zmax
    z[z&lt;0.0] += Zmax
    assert z.max() &lt;= Zmax
    assert z.min() &gt; 0.0
    thickness = Zmax / n_slab
    return np.floor(z / thickness).astype(int)


def _parse_profile(profile_log: str):
    # read the profile log file
    step = []
    area = []
    with open(profile_log, "r") as fr:
        for line in fr:
            if line.startswith("#"):
                step.append(int(line.split()[1]))
                area.append(float(line.split()[3]))
    step = np.array(step)
    area = np.array(area)
    profile_data = np.loadtxt(profile_log).reshape([len(step), -1, 4])
    return profile_data, step, area


def run_nemd(
    atoms, 
    save_dir: str, 
    temperature: float, 
    init_steps: int, 
    steps: int, 
    property: str, 
    n_slab: int, 
    n_swap: int, 
    swap_interval: int, 
    log_interval: int,
):
    # load the model
    estimator = Estimator(model_id=model_id, book_keeping=True)
    calc = ASECalculator(estimator)

    # create the save directory
    os.makedirs(f"{save_dir}", exist_ok=True)

    # define the print &amp; log function
    class PrintDyn:
        def __init__(self, dyn, logfile=None):
            self.dyn = dyn
            self.st = perf_counter()
            self.logfile = logfile
            if self.logfile is not None:
                with open(self.logfile, "w") as fd:
                    fd.write("# step E_tot E_pot density T elapsed_time\n")
        def __call__(self):
            dyn = self.dyn
            msg = (
                f"{dyn.get_number_of_steps(): 6d} {atoms.get_total_energy():.3f} {atoms.get_potential_energy():.3f} "
                f"{atoms.get_masses().sum() / units.kg / atoms.get_volume() * 1e27:.5f} "
                f"{atoms.get_temperature():.1f} {perf_counter() - self.st:.2f}"
            )
            print(msg)
            if self.logfile is not None:
                with open(self.logfile, "a") as fd:
                    fd.write(msg+"\n")

    # run md to equilibrate the system
    MaxwellBoltzmannDistribution(atoms, temperature_K=temperature)
    md = NPT(
        atoms, 
        timestep=units.fs, 
        temperature_K=temperature,
        externalstress=101325 * units.Pascal,
        mask = np.eye(3),
        ttime = 20.0 * units.fs,
        pfactor = 2e6 * units.GPa*(units.fs**2),
        logfile =None,
    )

    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md)
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=init_steps)
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/start.xsf")

    # run the RNEMD simulation
    ## set up the MD
    md = VelocityVerlet(atoms, timestep=units.fs)

    ## attach the RNEMD extension
    rnemd = RNEMDExtension(
        md,
        rnemd_type=property,
        n_slab=n_slab,
        n_swap=n_swap,
        swap_interval=swap_interval,
        log_interval=log_interval,
        logfile=f"{save_dir}/rnemd.log",
    )
    md.attach(rnemd)

    ## attach the RNEMD log extension
    if property == "viscosity":
        log_extension = VelocityLogExtension(
            md,
            n_slab=n_slab,
            freq=5,
            interval=log_interval,
            logfile=f"{save_dir}/profile.log"
        )
    elif property == "thermal_conductivity":
        log_extension = TemperatureLogExtension(
            md,
            n_slab=n_slab,
            freq=5,
            interval=log_interval,
            logfile=f"{save_dir}/profile.log"
        )

    md.attach(log_extension)

    ## run the simulation
    print_dyn=PrintDyn(md, logfile=f"{save_dir}/md.log")
    md.attach(print_dyn, interval=100)
    md.run(steps=steps)

    # save the final structure
    atoms.write(f"{save_dir}/final.xsf")
```
